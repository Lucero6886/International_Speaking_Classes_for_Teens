📱 My Phone & My Apps

A speaking lesson for Teens (A2 - B1)

🎯 Lesson Goals:

  • Talk about your daily phone habits and favourite apps.
  • Learn vocabulary related to smartphones and the internet.
  • Express opinions on screen time and social media.

🔥 1. Warm-Up

Let's get talking! Answer these quick questions. Click the button to see an example.

📱 What kind of phone do you have?

"I have an iPhone 11. It's black and I have a cool sticker on the case."

🎮 What is your favorite game to play on your phone?

"My favorite game right now is Roblox. I like playing it with my friends."

🤳 Do you prefer taking photos or making videos?

"I prefer making short videos for TikTok!"

💬 Who do you text the most every day?

"I text my best friend the most. We talk about school and funny memes."

🎧 Do you listen to music on your phone? What app do you use?

"Yes, I always listen to music on my way to school. I use Spotify."

📚 2. Vocabulary

Learn these words about phones and apps. Click to see an example sentence.

1. To download (v)

To copy a file, app, or picture from the internet to your phone.

"I need to download a new app for my math homework."
2. Notification (n)

A message that pops up on your screen to tell you something new.

"My phone buzzed because I got a notification from Instagram."
3. To post (v)

To put a picture, video, or text on the internet for others to see.

"I'm going to post this picture of my dog on my story."
4. Password (n)

A secret word or numbers you use to lock your phone or account.

"You should never tell anyone your Facebook password."
5. Screen time (n)

The total amount of time you spend looking at your phone or computer.

"My mom says my screen time is too high, so I have to read a book."
6. Useful (adj)

Helpful for doing a task; good for a purpose.

"Google Translate is very useful when I study English."
7. Follower (n)

A person who subscribes to your social media to see your posts.

"He makes really funny videos and has over a thousand followers."
8. Distracted (adj)

Not paying attention to what you should be doing.

"I couldn't finish my homework because I was distracted by my phone."

🗣️ 3. Let's Talk!

Take turns answering these questions. Use the buttons to get ideas or see sample answers if you get stuck!

Easy

1. What is the very first app you open when you wake up?

Ideas: Messages, Instagram, TikTok, an alarm app, a weather app.
Short: "The first app I open is my messages."
Long: "The first app I open is WhatsApp. I like to check if my friends sent me any messages while I was sleeping, and I check our class group chat for any news."
Easy

2. Do you use your phone to help with your school homework? How?

Ideas: Calculator, dictionary, taking photos of the whiteboard, searching Google.
Short: "Yes, I use it to search for information on Google."
Long: "Yes, my phone is very useful for homework. I use the calculator app for math, and I use an English dictionary app when I don't know a word. I also text my classmates if I need help."
Easy

3. What is your favourite emoji to use and why?

Ideas: 😂 (laughing), 💀 (skull/funny), ❤️ (heart), 😭 (crying/dramatic).
Short: "My favourite emoji is the laughing face 😂."
Long: "My favourite emoji is the skull emoji 💀. My friends and I use it all the time when something is really funny or embarrassing. It's better than saying 'haha'."
Medium

4. Do you prefer texting or sending voice notes? Why?

Ideas: Texting is quieter, voice notes are faster, voice shows emotion, texting is easier to read.
Short: "I prefer texting because it is quiet."
Long: "It depends! If I am in public or at school, I prefer texting because it is quiet. But if I have a long story to tell my friend, I send a voice note because typing takes too much time."
Medium

5. Have you ever dropped your phone and broken the screen? What happened?

Ideas: Dropped it on the street, it fell out of my pocket, parents were angry, had to pay to fix it.
Short: "Yes, I dropped it when I was running to the bus."
Long: "Yes, unfortunately! Last year, it fell out of my jacket pocket while I was riding my bike. The screen cracked completely. My parents were annoyed, and I had to use my pocket money to fix it."
Medium

6. How many photos do you have on your phone? What do you usually take photos of?

Ideas: Selfies with friends, pets, food, screenshots of homework, memes.
Short: "I have about 2,000 photos, mostly of my cat and my friends."
Long: "I probably have around 3,000 photos. A lot of them are just screenshots of funny memes or notes from the whiteboard at school. But I also take a lot of selfies with my friends when we go out."
Medium

7. What do you do if your phone battery dies when you are out?

Ideas: Panic, borrow a friend's phone, carry a power bank, enjoy not looking at a screen.
Short: "I always carry a portable charger, so it's not a problem."
Long: "I used to panic because I couldn't call my mom to pick me up. Now, I always carry a power bank in my backpack. If I forget it, I just have to borrow a friend's phone."
Opinion

8. Do you think teenagers spend too much time on their phones?

Ideas: Yes, it makes them lazy / No, it's how we socialize now / We get distracted from school.
Short: "Yes, I think we spend too much time looking at screens."
Long: "Yes, honestly I think we do. A lot of teenagers stay up late scrolling on TikTok, and then they are tired at school. We get easily distracted. But at the same time, it is the main way we talk to our friends, so it is hard to stop."
Opinion

9. Should students be allowed to use their phones in the classroom?

Ideas: No, they play games / Yes, for educational apps like Kahoot / Only for emergencies.
Short: "No, because students will just play games instead of listening."
Long: "I think they should only be allowed if the teacher asks us to use them. For example, if we are playing a learning game like Quizlet. If we can use them all the time, everyone will just chat or play games and no one will study."
Opinion

10. Is it a good idea for families to have "no phone" rules during dinner?

Ideas: Good for family bonding, prevents ignoring each other, parents use phones too!
Short: "Yes, because dinner is a time to talk with your family."
Long: "I think it is a great idea. If everyone is looking at their screen, no one talks about their day. Dinner is the perfect time for family bonding. But I think parents need to follow the rule too, not just the kids!"

🎭 4. Role-Play Scenarios

Choose a partner and act out these real-life situations!

Scenario A: The New App

Student A (Teen): You want to download a new social media app that all your friends have. You need to ask your parent for permission.

Student B (Parent): You are worried about the app. You think it's a waste of time and dangerous.

  • "Mom/Dad, can I please download this app? Everyone has it!"
  • "I don't know, you already spend too much screen time."
  • "I promise I will only use it for 30 minutes a day."
  • "Is your account going to be private?"

Scenario B: Tech Support

Student A (Grandparent): You just got your first smartphone. You want to learn how to send a photo in a message, but you are very confused.

Student B (Teen): You are trying to patiently teach your grandparent how to use the phone.

  • "Where did the picture go? I touched the screen and it vanished!"
  • "Don't worry Grandma, just press this little plus button here."
  • "What is an app? Is that the blue square?"
  • "Yes! Now tap the photo you want to send."
